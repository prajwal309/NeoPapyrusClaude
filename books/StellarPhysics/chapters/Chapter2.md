# Chapter 2 — Gravity and Hydrostatic Equilibrium

---

## 2.1 The Battle Inside a Star

<div data-level="101">

At the heart of every star lies a constant struggle.

Gravity is always pulling inward, trying to collapse the star.  
Pressure pushes outward, trying to hold it up.

A star exists because these two forces are in balance.

If gravity suddenly became stronger, the star would shrink.  
If pressure increased, the star would expand.

This delicate balance is what allows stars to shine steadily for billions of years.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Gravity arises from the star’s mass:

- Every layer of gas is pulled inward by the mass beneath it.

Pressure arises from:

- Thermal motion of particles (gas pressure)  
- Radiation pressure (important in massive stars)

At equilibrium:

- The inward gravitational force is exactly balanced by the outward pressure gradient.

This condition is known as **hydrostatic equilibrium**.

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

Hydrostatic equilibrium is a local condition:

Each layer of the star adjusts so that forces balance.

This is not a static system — it is a dynamic equilibrium:

- Energy is continuously generated in the core  
- Energy flows outward  
- Pressure continuously adjusts to maintain balance  

Any perturbation (e.g., contraction) triggers a response:

- Contraction → heating → increased pressure → expansion  

This self-regulating behavior stabilizes stars.

</div>

---

## 2.2 The Equation of Hydrostatic Equilibrium

<div data-level="101">

To describe this balance mathematically, we look at a thin shell inside the star.

The pressure below pushes it up.  
Gravity pulls it down.

The difference between these forces determines whether the shell moves.

In a stable star, they cancel exactly.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

The equation of hydrostatic equilibrium is:

\[
\frac{dP}{dr} = - \frac{G M(r)\rho(r)}{r^2}
\]

where:

- \(P\) = pressure  
- \(r\) = radius  
- \(M(r)\) = mass enclosed within radius \(r\)  
- \(\rho(r)\) = density  
- \(G\) = gravitational constant  

This equation states:

> Pressure must decrease outward to counteract gravity.

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

This equation is derived by considering force balance on a spherical shell:

- Gravitational force: \( \propto \frac{G M(r)}{r^2} \)  
- Pressure gradient force: \( \frac{dP}{dr} \)

It couples with:

- Mass conservation:  
  \[
  \frac{dM}{dr} = 4\pi r^2 \rho
  \]

- Energy transport equations  

Together, these define the internal structure of a star.

Boundary conditions:

- \(M(0) = 0\) at the center  
- \(P(R) \approx 0\) at the surface  

</div>

---

## 2.3 Order-of-Magnitude Estimates for Stellar Interiors

<div data-level="101">

Even without solving complex equations, we can estimate what happens inside a star.

For example:

- The center must be extremely hot  
- The pressure must be enormous  

Otherwise, gravity would crush the star.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Using simple scaling arguments, we can estimate central pressure:

\[
P_c \sim \frac{G M^2}{R^4}
\]

and central temperature:

\[
T_c \sim \frac{G M \mu}{R k_B}
\]

where:

- \(M\) = stellar mass  
- \(R\) = stellar radius  
- \(\mu\) = mean molecular weight  
- \(k_B\) = Boltzmann constant  

For the Sun:

- \(T_c \sim 10^7 \, \text{K}\)  
- \(P_c \sim 10^{16} \, \text{Pa}\)

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

These estimates arise from combining:

- Hydrostatic equilibrium  
- Ideal gas law:  
  \[
  P = \frac{\rho k_B T}{\mu m_H}
  \]

They reveal key insights:

- More massive stars → higher core temperatures  
- Smaller radii → higher pressures  

This explains why:

- Massive stars burn fuel faster  
- Low-mass stars evolve slowly  

Order-of-magnitude reasoning is a powerful tool before full numerical modeling.

</div>

---

## 2.4 Why Stars Don’t Collapse

<div data-level="101">

Gravity is always trying to collapse a star.

So why doesn’t it succeed?

Because as the star contracts, it heats up.

Hotter gas creates more pressure.  
That pressure pushes back against gravity.

This feedback keeps the star stable.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

This stability arises from:

- **Thermal pressure response**  
- **Energy generation in the core**

If the star contracts:

1. Temperature increases  
2. Fusion rates increase  
3. Energy output increases  
4. Pressure increases  
5. Contraction stops  

This is a **negative feedback loop**.

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

This stability can be understood through the **virial theorem**:

\[
2K + U = 0
\]

where:

- \(K\) = thermal energy  
- \(U\) = gravitational potential energy  

Implications:

- As a star loses energy, it contracts and heats up  
- As it gains energy, it expands and cools  

This counterintuitive behavior stabilizes stars.

Breakdown of this balance leads to:

- Stellar collapse (e.g., supernova progenitors)  
- Degenerate objects (white dwarfs, neutron stars)  

</div>

---

## Summary

- Stars exist due to balance between gravity and pressure  
- Hydrostatic equilibrium governs internal structure  
- Simple scaling laws estimate core conditions  
- Feedback mechanisms prevent collapse  
- The virial theorem explains stellar stability
