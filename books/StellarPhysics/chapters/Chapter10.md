# Chapter 10 — Formation of Stars

---

## 10.1 From Clouds to Stars

<div data-level="101">

Stars are born in darkness.

Before a star shines, it begins as a cold cloud of gas and dust drifting through space.  
Under the right conditions, gravity takes over — and the cloud begins to collapse.

Over time, this collapse leads to the birth of a star.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Star formation occurs in **molecular clouds**:

- Cold (\(\sim 10\) K)  
- Dense regions of gas (mostly \( \mathrm{H}_2 \))  
- Often structured into filaments and clumps  

Gravity competes with:

- Thermal pressure  
- Turbulence  
- Magnetic fields  

When gravity dominates, collapse begins.

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

Star formation is a multi-scale process:

- Giant molecular clouds (10–100 pc)  
- Dense cores (~0.1 pc)  
- Protostellar collapse (~AU scales)  

Key physics includes:

- Self-gravity  
- Radiative cooling  
- Magnetohydrodynamics (MHD)  
- Turbulence  

The collapse is not smooth — it is highly structured and dynamic.

</div>

---

## 10.2 Jeans Instability

<div data-level="101">

Not every cloud forms stars.

Only clouds that are massive enough — or cold enough — can collapse.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

The condition for collapse is given by the **Jeans criterion**.

A cloud becomes unstable when its mass exceeds the **Jeans mass**:

\[
M_J \sim \left( \frac{k_B T}{G \mu m_H} \right)^{3/2} \rho^{-1/2}
\]

Key idea:

- High temperature → resists collapse  
- High density → promotes collapse  

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

The Jeans instability arises from perturbation analysis of a self-gravitating medium.

Characteristic scales:

- **Jeans length**:
  \[
  \lambda_J \sim \left( \frac{k_B T}{G \mu m_H \rho} \right)^{1/2}
  \]

- **Jeans mass**:
  \[
  M_J \propto T^{3/2} \rho^{-1/2}
  \]

Implications:

- Cold, dense regions fragment into multiple cores  
- Sets characteristic mass scale for star formation  

In reality, turbulence and magnetic fields modify these simple criteria.

</div>

---

## 10.3 Protostars and Accretion

<div data-level="101">

As a cloud collapses, material gathers at the center.

A dense object forms — a **protostar**.

But the story doesn’t end there.

Gas continues to fall inward, feeding the growing star.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

During collapse:

- A central protostar forms  
- Surrounding gas forms an **accretion disk**  
- Material spirals inward  

Angular momentum prevents direct collapse, leading to disk formation.

Accretion rates determine how quickly the star grows.

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

Protostellar evolution involves:

- Accretion luminosity:
  \[
  L_{\mathrm{acc}} \sim \frac{G M \dot{M}}{R}
  \]

- Disk evolution and angular momentum transport  
- Outflows and jets (which remove angular momentum)

Magnetic fields play a key role in:

- Launching jets  
- Regulating accretion  

Protostars are often deeply embedded and observable primarily in infrared.

</div>

---

## 10.4 Pre-Main-Sequence Evolution

<div data-level="101">

Before a star begins fusion, it goes through a phase of contraction.

It is not yet a true star — but it is getting there.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

During the **pre-main-sequence phase**:

- The star contracts under gravity  
- Temperature increases  
- Luminosity changes over time  

Energy comes from gravitational contraction (not fusion).

Eventually:

- Core temperature becomes high enough for hydrogen fusion  
- The star enters the **main sequence**

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

Pre-main-sequence evolution follows:

- **Hayashi track** (fully convective stars)  
- **Henyey track** (radiative cores develop)

The timescale is given by the **Kelvin–Helmholtz timescale**:

\[
t_{\mathrm{KH}} \sim \frac{G M^2}{R L}
\]

Key transitions:

- Onset of hydrogen burning  
- Structural changes (convective → radiative zones)

Observational signatures include:

- Infrared excess (from disks)  
- Variability  
- Emission lines  

</div>

---

## 10.5 A Physical Picture

<div data-level="101">

A star begins as a cloud.

Gravity pulls it together.  
Heat builds up.  
Eventually, it ignites.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Star formation is a balance between:

- Gravity (collapse)  
- Pressure and temperature (resistance)  

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

The formation of stars connects:

- Interstellar medium physics  
- Fluid dynamics  
- Radiation and thermodynamics  

It sets the initial conditions for:

- Stellar evolution  
- Planet formation  
- Galactic structure  

</div>

---

## Summary

- Stars form from collapsing molecular clouds  
- Jeans instability determines when collapse occurs  
- Protostars grow through accretion  
- Pre-main-sequence evolution prepares stars for fusion  
- Star formation is a complex, multi-physics process
