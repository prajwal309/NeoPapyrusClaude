# Chapter 7 — Stellar Atmospheres and Spectra

## 7.1 The Light We See

<div data-level="101">

Everything we know about stars comes from their light.

We cannot touch them.  
We cannot visit them.  

But we can **analyze their light**.

The outermost layer of a star — its **atmosphere** — is where this light is formed.

Understanding stellar atmospheres means understanding how light is created, absorbed, and modified before it reaches us.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

The stellar atmosphere is the region where:

- Photons escape into space  
- Optical depth transitions from opaque to transparent  

The observed spectrum carries information about:

- Temperature  
- Composition  
- Surface gravity  
- Velocity fields  

Thus, stellar atmospheres are the interface between **internal physics** and **observations**.

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

The atmosphere is modeled using:

- Radiative transfer  
- Opacity sources  
- Thermodynamic structure  

Key challenges include:

- Non-local thermodynamic equilibrium (non-LTE)  
- 3D structure and convection  
- Stellar heterogeneity (spots, faculae)

Modern stellar spectroscopy relies on detailed atmosphere models (e.g., PHOENIX, ATLAS).

</div>

---

## 7.2 Radiative Transfer Basics

<div data-level="101">

Light does not simply escape a star.

As it travels outward, it can:

- Be absorbed  
- Be re-emitted  
- Change direction  

This process is called **radiative transfer**.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Radiative transfer is described by:

\[
\frac{dI_\nu}{ds} = -\kappa_\nu I_\nu + j_\nu
\]

where:

- \(I_\nu\) = intensity  
- \(\kappa_\nu\) = opacity  
- \(j_\nu\) = emissivity  

This equation tracks how light changes as it moves through the atmosphere.

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

In local thermodynamic equilibrium (LTE), the source function becomes:

\[
S_\nu = \frac{j_\nu}{\kappa_\nu} = B_\nu(T)
\]

where \(B_\nu(T)\) is the Planck function.

Solutions to the radiative transfer equation determine:

- Emergent spectra  
- Limb darkening  
- Line profiles  

Departures from LTE require full statistical equilibrium calculations.

</div>

---

## 7.3 Blackbody vs Real Stars

<div data-level="101">

If stars were perfect emitters, they would glow like ideal objects called **blackbodies**.

A blackbody emits light smoothly across all wavelengths.

But real stars are not perfect.

Their light contains features — dips and patterns.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

A blackbody spectrum is given by the Planck function:

\[
B_\nu(T)
\]

It depends only on temperature.

Real stellar spectra deviate due to:

- Absorption lines  
- Opacity variations  
- Atmospheric structure  

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

Deviations from blackbody arise from:

- Frequency-dependent opacity  
- Line blanketing  
- Temperature gradients  

These effects modify the emergent spectrum:

\[
F_\nu = \int I_\nu \, d\Omega
\]

Understanding these deviations allows us to extract:

- Chemical abundances  
- Stellar parameters  
- Atmospheric conditions  

</div>

---

## 7.4 Absorption Lines and Line Formation

<div data-level="101">

If you spread starlight into a rainbow, you will see dark lines.

These are **absorption lines**.

They occur when atoms in the star absorb specific wavelengths of light.

Each element leaves its own fingerprint.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Line formation depends on:

- Atomic transitions  
- Temperature  
- Density  

When photons interact with atoms:

- Bound-bound transitions → spectral lines  
- Bound-free transitions → continuum absorption  

Line depth and shape depend on:

- Abundance  
- Pressure broadening  
- Doppler shifts  

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

Line formation is governed by:

- Radiative transfer  
- Statistical equilibrium  

Line profiles include:

- Natural broadening  
- Thermal Doppler broadening  
- Pressure (collisional) broadening  

The equivalent width measures line strength.

Spectral lines are used to derive:

- Elemental abundances  
- Radial velocities  
- Stellar rotation  

In exoplanet studies, line contamination from stellar heterogeneity is a key challenge.

</div>

---

## 7.5 Spectral Classification (OBAFGKM)

<div data-level="101">

Stars come in different colors:

- Blue stars are hot  
- Red stars are cool  

Astronomers classify stars into types:

> O, B, A, F, G, K, M

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Spectral classes correspond to temperature:

- **O** — very hot, blue  
- **B** — hot  
- **A** — strong hydrogen lines  
- **F** — moderate temperature  
- **G** — Sun-like  
- **K** — cooler  
- **M** — cool, red  

This sequence reflects changes in:

- Ionization states  
- Line strengths  

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

Spectral classification is based on:

- Line ratios  
- Ionization balance  
- Molecular features (in cool stars)

Temperature range:

- O: \(> 30{,}000\) K  
- M: \(< 3{,}500\) K  

Modern classification includes:

- Luminosity classes (I–V)  
- Metallicity effects  

Spectral typing connects directly to:

- Stellar evolution  
- Population studies  
- Exoplanet host characterization  

</div>

## 7.6 A Physical Perspective

<div data-level="101">

A star’s light is not just brightness.

It is a message.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Each feature in the spectrum tells us something:

- Temperature  
- Composition  
- Motion  

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

Stellar spectra are the foundation of astrophysics.

They connect:

- Microphysics (atoms, transitions)  
- Macrophysics (stellar structure)  

They also form the basis of:

- Transmission spectroscopy  
- Stellar contamination modeling  
- Population studies  

</div>

## Summary

- Stellar atmospheres produce the light we observe  
- Radiative transfer governs how light propagates  
- Real stars deviate from blackbody emission  
- Absorption lines reveal composition and physics  
- Spectral classification organizes stars by temperature
