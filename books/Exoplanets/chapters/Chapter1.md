If you happen to live away from significant sources of artificial light, the night sky reveals a dense stellar field—thousands of point sources distributed across the celestial sphere. This observational experience has historically motivated fundamental questions about the nature and distribution of astronomical objects.

For much of recorded history, the sky was interpreted as largely static. In the geocentric framework, stars were considered fixed on a celestial sphere, while planets (“wanderers”) exhibited more complex, yet still predictable, motion. Within this paradigm, the Solar System—comprising the Sun, planets, moons, minor bodies, and comets—was implicitly assumed to be unique.

The hypothesis that other stars might host planetary systems dates back to early philosophical speculation (e.g., Democritus, Epicurus), but remained untestable for centuries. The transition from philosophical conjecture to empirical science required a sequence of conceptual and observational advances.

A major inflection point occurred in the 16th century with the heliocentric model proposed by Nicolaus Copernicus, which placed the Sun at the center of the Solar System. Subsequent work by Kepler (elliptical orbits) and Newton (gravitational dynamics) established a quantitative framework for celestial mechanics. These developments demonstrated that the motions of planets could be explained through universal physical laws, rather than ad hoc geometric constructions.

Over the following centuries, astronomical observations progressively expanded the known scale of the universe. The Sun was recognized as an ordinary star within the Milky Way, and later, the Milky Way itself was shown to be one galaxy among many. This sequence of discoveries reinforced a key principle: there is no a priori reason to consider the Solar System as exceptional.

From a statistical standpoint, if star formation is a ubiquitous process governed by common physical mechanisms—namely gravitational collapse, angular momentum conservation, and disk formation—then planet formation should arise as a natural byproduct. This expectation leads to a testable hypothesis: planetary systems should be common throughout the galaxy.

Only in the late 20th century did observational techniques—primarily radial velocity and transit photometry—provide the sensitivity required to detect planets around other stars. These detections transformed the question from speculative to empirical.

We now understand that planets are not rare anomalies but a frequent outcome of star formation.

This realization reframes a fundamental question:

Given a population of stars, what is the distribution of planetary systems—and how do we infer it from incomplete and noisy data?

This is, at its core, a statistical problem.


✦ A Turning Point in Discovery

That changed in the 1990s.

With advances in precision spectroscopy and photometry, astronomers finally detected the first planets orbiting other stars. The discovery was not subtle. The first confirmed detections revealed worlds unlike anything in our Solar System: giant planets orbiting scorchingly close to their stars. Hot Jupiters were not predicted by classical Solar System theory — yet there they were.

These planets challenged long-standing assumptions about planet formation and orbital architecture. Instead of distant, cold gas giants like Jupiter, astronomers found massive worlds completing orbits in just a few days, bathed in intense stellar radiation.

The first confirmed exoplanets were discovered in 1992 by Aleksander Wolszczan and Dale Frail, orbiting a pulsar (PSR B1257+12). These were exotic, dead-star systems — fascinating, but far from Sun-like.

The breakthrough for “normal” stars came in 1995, when Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz discovered 51 Pegasi b, the first hot Jupiter orbiting a Sun-like star.

They used the radial velocity method, detecting tiny Doppler shifts in the star’s spectrum caused by the gravitational pull of the planet.

This discovery was so transformative that Mayor and Queloz were awarded the 2019 Nobel Prize in Physics.

✦ Seeing the Unseen

Soon after, astronomers achieved something even more remarkable: the direct imaging of exoplanets.

Systems such as HR 8799 revealed multiple massive planets glowing in infrared light, physically separated from their host star in telescope images. These observations required sophisticated techniques — including coronagraphy and adaptive optics — to block out the overwhelming glare of the star.

For the first time, planets around other stars were not just inferred — they were seen.

Exoplanet science has transformed astronomy. We are no longer studying a single planetary system — we are studying planetary systems as a population. We now ask statistical questions:

- How common are Earth-sized planets?

- What determines whether a planet keeps an atmosphere?

## 1.2 The First Discoveries

The first confirmed exoplanets were discovered in 1992—not around a Sun-like star, but around a pulsar. These planets orbited the neutron star PSR B1257+12, showing that planets can form—or survive—even in extreme environments.

In 1995, the first exoplanet orbiting a Sun-like star was discovered: **51 Pegasi b**. This planet was a "hot Jupiter"—a gas giant orbiting extremely close to its host star. Its discovery challenged long-standing theories of planetary formation and sparked a revolution in astronomy.

Since then, thousands of exoplanets have been discovered using a variety of detection methods, revealing a universe filled with planetary diversity.

---

## 1.3 How Do We Detect Exoplanets?

Because exoplanets are faint and lie extremely close to bright stars, we rarely see them directly. Instead, we detect them indirectly through their effects on their host stars.

### 1.3.1 The Transit Method

When a planet passes in front of its star, it blocks a small fraction of starlight. This produces a periodic dip in brightness known as a **transit**.

From transits, astronomers can measure:
- The planet’s radius  
- Its orbital period  
- Its distance from the star (via Kepler’s Third Law)  

Space telescopes like *Kepler* and *TESS* have discovered thousands of planets using this technique.

<div data-level="201">

A transit occurs when the orbital plane of a planet is aligned such that the planet crosses the stellar disk as seen from Earth. During the transit, the observed stellar flux temporarily decreases.

To first order, the depth of the transit is determined by the ratio of the areas of the planet and the star,

$$
\delta \approx \left(\frac{R_p}{R_*}\right)^2 ,
$$

where $R_p$ is the planetary radius and $R_*$ is the stellar radius. Because stars are much larger than planets, typical transit depths are small. For example, an Earth-sized planet transiting a Sun-like star produces a dip of only about **80 parts per million (ppm)**.

The **duration** of the transit depends on the orbital geometry and stellar properties. For a circular orbit, the approximate transit duration is

$$
T \sim \frac{P}{\pi} \frac{R_*}{a},
$$

where $P$ is the orbital period and $a$ is the orbital semi-major axis.

Combining the orbital period measured from repeated transits with the stellar mass allows astronomers to infer the orbital distance using **Kepler’s Third Law**,

$$
P^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 a^3}{G M_*}.
$$

More detailed modeling of transit light curves also reveals additional parameters such as:

- the **orbital inclination**
- the **impact parameter** (how centrally the planet crosses the star)
- the **stellar limb darkening profile**

These quantities are inferred by fitting transit models to the observed light curve.

</div>

### 1.3.2 The Radial Velocity Method

A planet’s gravity pulls on its star, causing the star to wobble slightly. This motion shifts the star’s spectral lines due to the Doppler effect.

From radial velocity measurements, astronomers can determine:
- The planet’s minimum mass  
- Its orbital period  
- Orbital eccentricity  

Together, transit and radial velocity measurements allow us to estimate a planet’s **density**, which provides clues about its composition.

## 1.4 A Zoo of Worlds

Exoplanets come in many varieties:

- **Hot Jupiters** — Gas giants orbiting extremely close to their stars  
- **Warm Neptunes** — Intermediate-mass planets with thick atmospheres  
- **Super-Earths** — Rocky planets larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune  
- **Mini-Neptunes** — Low-density planets with significant gaseous envelopes  
- **Temperate terrestrial planets** — Potentially rocky worlds in the habitable zone  

One of the most surprising discoveries is that the most common types of planets in the galaxy—super-Earths and mini-Neptunes—do not exist in our Solar System.

Our planetary system is not typical.

---

## 1.5 Why Exoplanets Matter

Studying exoplanets helps us answer some of the most profound scientific questions:

- How do planetary systems form and evolve?
- How common are Earth-like planets?
- What determines planetary atmospheres?
- Are we alone in the universe?

Exoplanet research connects astrophysics, planetary science, atmospheric chemistry, and even biology. It represents one of the most interdisciplinary and rapidly evolving fields in modern science.

---

## 1.6 The Era of Atmospheric Characterization

We are no longer limited to detecting planets—we can now probe their atmospheres.

During transits, some starlight passes through a planet’s atmosphere. Different molecules absorb light at specific wavelengths, leaving fingerprints in the observed spectrum. This technique, known as **transmission spectroscopy**, allows astronomers to detect:

- Water vapor  
- Methane  
- Carbon dioxide  
- Clouds and hazes  
- Temperature structures  

With telescopes like the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), we are entering an era where detailed atmospheric studies are becoming routine.

### The TRAPPIST-1 System

One of the most remarkable planetary systems discovered in recent years is **TRAPPIST-1**, a small and extremely cool star located about 40 light-years from Earth in the constellation Aquarius. Despite being only about 9% the mass of the Sun, this star hosts a tightly packed system of **seven Earth-sized planets**.

The planets were discovered using the **transit method**, in which astronomers measure the small dimming of a star’s light as a planet passes in front of it. The initial detection came from the **TRAPPIST telescope** in Chile, and later observations with larger telescopes and the *Spitzer Space Telescope* confirmed that the system contains seven planets.

What makes TRAPPIST-1 particularly fascinating is the architecture of its planetary system. All seven planets orbit extremely close to their star, completing an orbit in only **1.5 to 19 days**. Despite these short orbital periods, the low luminosity of the star means that several of the planets receive amounts of stellar radiation comparable to that received by Earth.

Three of the planets—TRAPPIST-1e, TRAPPIST-1f, and TRAPPIST-1g—lie within the star’s **habitable zone**, the region where temperatures could allow liquid water to exist on a planetary surface under suitable atmospheric conditions.

The planets are also arranged in a striking **resonant chain**, meaning that their orbital periods follow simple ratios with one another. This configuration likely formed through migration within the protoplanetary disk during the early stages of the system’s evolution.

Because the planets are roughly Earth-sized and orbit a small star, the TRAPPIST-1 system has become a major target for atmospheric studies with modern observatories such as the **James Webb Space Telescope**. By studying their atmospheres, astronomers hope to learn whether these worlds possess thick atmospheres, volatile-rich envelopes, or potentially habitable environments.

TRAPPIST-1 therefore represents one of the most promising laboratories for studying rocky planets beyond the Solar System.

## 1.7 A Population Perspective


<div data-level="101">

When the first exoplanets were discovered in the 1990s, each one felt extraordinary. Every new planet revealed a system unlike our own: giant planets orbiting extremely close to their stars, planets with eccentric orbits, and worlds far larger than any in our Solar System.

But the real revolution began when astronomers started discovering **many planets**. Instead of studying a single world at a time, scientists could compare hundreds or thousands of planetary systems.

With large numbers of planets, patterns begin to emerge. Astronomers can ask questions such as:

- Do small stars host smaller planets?
- Are giant planets more common around certain types of stars?
- Do planetary systems tend to form similar architectures?

By studying many systems together, exoplanet science becomes less about individual discoveries and more about understanding **how planetary systems behave as a population**.

</div>

---

<div data-level="201">

Large exoplanet surveys—such as those conducted by the *Kepler* and *TESS* space telescopes—have revealed thousands of planets with a wide range of sizes, orbital periods, and host-star properties. These datasets allow astronomers to investigate statistical relationships between planets and their environments.

One of the most important discoveries is the connection between **stellar metallicity** and the occurrence of giant planets. Stars enriched in heavy elements are significantly more likely to host gas giants. This supports theoretical models in which massive planetary cores must form before rapidly accreting gas from the protoplanetary disk.

Population studies also show that planetary properties correlate with stellar mass. For example, small M-dwarf stars commonly host compact systems of short-period rocky planets. In contrast, more massive stars often host larger and more widely spaced planets.

By examining large samples, astronomers can also investigate how stellar activity influences observational measurements. Magnetic phenomena such as starspots and faculae can affect both photometric and spectroscopic observations, introducing biases that must be carefully modeled when interpreting planetary atmospheres.

These statistical approaches transform exoplanet science from a collection of individual discoveries into a framework for understanding **planet formation and evolution on a galactic scale**.

</div>

---

<div data-level="301">

At the research level, population studies aim to infer the underlying distributions that govern planetary formation and system architecture. Observed exoplanet samples are affected by strong observational selection effects: for example, transit surveys preferentially detect short-period planets, while radial velocity surveys are more sensitive to massive companions.

To recover the intrinsic planetary population, astronomers apply completeness corrections and hierarchical statistical models that account for detection biases. These analyses have revealed several key features of the planetary population.

One of the most striking is the **radius valley**—a deficit of planets with radii near $\sim1.8\,R_\oplus$ separating rocky super-Earths from volatile-rich sub-Neptunes. This feature likely reflects atmospheric loss processes such as photoevaporation or core-powered mass loss.

Population analyses have also identified correlations between planetary occurrence rates and stellar properties, including mass, metallicity, and age. These trends provide powerful constraints on models of planet formation, disk evolution, and dynamical migration.

More recently, population-level studies have begun incorporating stellar heterogeneity and observational systematics into atmospheric analyses. For instance, stellar surface inhomogeneities can imprint wavelength-dependent signals in transmission spectra, complicating the interpretation of atmospheric features across large samples of planets.

Through these statistical approaches, exoplanet science is evolving toward a unified picture in which individual planetary systems are understood as outcomes of broader physical processes shaping planetary populations throughout the Milky Way.

</div>


## 1.8 Looking Ahead

The study of exoplanets is still in its infancy. Yet even in the few decades since the first discoveries, our understanding of planetary systems has expanded dramatically. What was once speculation has become a rapidly advancing field of observational astronomy.

In the coming decades, new instruments and observatories will allow us to characterize distant worlds with unprecedented precision. Improved measurements of planetary masses and radii will refine our understanding of planetary compositions and internal structures. High-resolution atmospheric spectroscopy will reveal the chemical makeup of alien atmospheres, allowing us to detect molecules such as water vapor, carbon dioxide, and methane.

At the same time, advances in direct imaging will enable astronomers to isolate the faint light of planets from the overwhelming glare of their host stars. This technique may allow us to observe Earth-sized planets orbiting nearby stars and study them as individual worlds.

Perhaps most profound is the possibility of searching for **biosignatures**—chemical or spectral indicators that may suggest the presence of life. Detecting such signals would represent one of the most significant scientific discoveries in human history.

Not long ago, the question that motivated this field was a simple and philosophical one:

*Are there other worlds?*

Today we know that the answer is yes. Planets are common throughout the galaxy.

The questions we now ask are deeper:

*What are these worlds like?*  
*How do planetary systems form and evolve?*  
*Under what conditions can life arise?*

This book explores these questions by examining how planets are discovered, how their properties are measured, and how their atmospheres and environments are studied.

The universe, once imagined as largely empty between the stars, is now understood to be rich with planetary systems.

The night sky is not only a field of stars.

It is filled with planets.
